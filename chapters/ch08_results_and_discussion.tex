    \chapter{Results and Discussion} \label{chap:resultsanddiscussion}

    The evaluation of the proposed method using the experiments and evaluation metrics discussed in the previous chapter is presented in this chapter. The performance comparison of the proposed \gls{cvo} approach and the baseline odometry against the Vicon-based ground truth is discussed. This chapter is organized based on the terrain types used in the experiments. The final part of this chapter presents the discussion of the results obtained from the experiments. 
    
    \section{Flat Floor Terrain Experiments} \label{sec:flatFloorTerrainResults}   
    
    The evaluation results from the datasets presented in Section~\ref{subsec:exp_flatFloorTerrain} are discussed in this section. A total of 53 datasets were collected on flat floor terrain experiments. Since presenting all datasets would make the chapter repetitive, a set of representative datasets is selected for detailed discussion. The combined plots show the ground truth, baseline odometry, and the contact velocity odometry data together in a single plot. The following subsections present selected combined 2D trajectory plots, \texttt{z}-position comparison plots, and translational and rotational \gls{rpe} plots for the baseline odometry and contact velocity odometry. Where relevant, translational and rotational \gls{ape} plots are also presented. For each selected dataset, a summary table is provided with the final pose error, translational and rotational drift rates, and travel distance error.
    
    The representative datasets are selected to include different trajectory types, such as straight-line motion, circular motion, and complex trajectories. This selection helps to discuss the performance of the proposed method on simple and complex trajectories.  Unless stated otherwise, the same color coding is used across all plots. The ground truth trajectory is shown in blue, the baseline odometry trajectory is shown in green, and the contact velocity odometry trajectory is shown in red. The yaw orientations are shown as heading arrows along the trajectories using the same respective colors.
    
    \subsection{Straight-Line Motion}
    
    The straight-line motion datasets include the experiments where the rover moves in a straight-line without any turns. Three straight-line motion types are included on flat floor terrain experiments. The straight-line motion datasets are: \texttt{DATA\_02\_YY}, \texttt{DATA\_03\_YY}, and \texttt{DATA\_04\_YY}, where \texttt{YY} represents the iteration number. The evaluation results of these datasets are presented in the following subsections.

    From Figure~\ref{fig:straight_line_motion_dataset_02_03_trajectory}, it can be observed that the baseline odometry trajectory closely follows the ground truth, while the \gls{cvo} trajectory shows an initial yaw deviation, which is reflected in the deviation of the X--Y trajectory.
    
    From Table~\ref{tab:odometry_metrics_data-02-03}, it can be observed that the \gls{cvo} approach has a higher travel distance error, final position error, and translational and rotational drift rates compared to the baseline odometry. A negative travel distance error indicates that the estimated travel distance is smaller than the ground truth travel distance. The \gls{rmse} of the translational and rotational \gls{rpe} drift is higher for the \gls{cvo} compared to the baseline odometry, as shown in Figures~\ref{fig:straight_line_motion_dataset_02_03_RPE_translational} and~\ref{fig:straight_line_motion_dataset_02_03_RPE_rotational}. Notably, the \texttt{z}-position plot shown in Figure~\ref{fig:straight_line_motion_dataset_02_03_z_position} indicates that the \gls{cvo} approach tracks the ground truth better than the baseline odometry. Based on the evaluation results, when the rover moves in a straight-line forward without any turns or complex maneuvers, the baseline odometry performs better than the \gls{cvo} approach.

    \subsection{Flat Floor Terrain Evaluation Summary} \label{subsec:flatFloorEvalSummary}
    
    Overall, the straight-line flat floor experiments show that the baseline odometry performs better than the \gls{cvo} approach in terms of travel distance error and translational \gls{rpe} drift. This shows that, in simple straight-line motion, the baseline approch is sufficient to estimate the rover's pose. Even though the \gls{cvo} approach performs better in terms of \texttt{z}-position estimation, the overall performance is not better than the baseline odometry.
