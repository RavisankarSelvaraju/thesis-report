    \chapter{Results and Discussion} \label{chap:resultsanddiscussion}

    The evaluation of the proposed method using the experiments and evaluation metrics discussed in the previous chapter is presented in this chapter. The performance comparison of the proposed \gls{cvo} approach and the baseline odometry against the Vicon-based ground truth is discussed. This chapter is organized based on the terrain types used in the experiments. The final part of this chapter presents the discussion of the results obtained from the experiments. The flat floor terrain experiments compare the performance of the baseline odometry and the proposed \gls{cvo} approach only focusing on the \gls{cvo} estimates. The slip elimination part is not discussed in flat floor terrain. The crater terrain experiments explicitly discuss the slip event and the comparison of \gls{cvo} approach with and without slip elimination. 
    
    \section{Flat Floor Terrain Experiments} \label{sec:flatFloorTerrainResults}   
    
    The evaluation results from the datasets presented in Section~\ref{subsec:exp_flatFloorTerrain} are discussed in this section. A total of 53 datasets were collected on flat floor terrain experiments. Since presenting all datasets would make the chapter repetitive, a set of representative datasets is selected for detailed discussion. The combined plots show the ground truth, baseline odometry, and the contact velocity odometry data together in a single plot. The following subsections present selected combined 2D trajectory plots, \texttt{z}-position comparison plots, and translational and rotational \gls{rpe} plots for the baseline odometry and contact velocity odometry. Where relevant, translational and rotational \gls{ape} plots are also presented. For each selected dataset, a summary table is provided with the final pose error, translational and rotational drift rates, and travel distance error.
    
    The representative datasets are selected to include different trajectory types, such as straight-line motion, circular motion, and complex trajectories. This selection helps to discuss the performance of the proposed method on simple and complex trajectories. Unless stated otherwise, the same color coding is used across all plots. The ground truth trajectory is shown in blue, the baseline odometry trajectory is shown in green, and the contact velocity odometry trajectory is shown in red. The yaw orientations are shown as heading arrows along the trajectories using the same respective colors.

    \subsection{Evaluation Summary of Flat Floor Terrain Experiments} \label{subsec:flatFloorEvalSummary}

    The straight-line flat floor experiments show that the baseline odometry performs better than the \gls{cvo} approach in terms of travel distance error and translational \gls{rpe} drift. This shows that the baseline approach is sufficient for simple low-speed straight-line motion on flat floor terrain. Even though the \gls{cvo} approach captures the variation pattern of the \texttt{z}-position better in some cases, the overall performance is not better than the baseline odometry.

    The circular motion flat floor experiments show that the rotational \gls{rpe} drift is slightly better for the \gls{cvo} approach compared to the baseline odometry. This shows that the \gls{cvo} approach can provide better local rotational estimates in circular motion. However, the yaw \gls{rmse} is still lower for the baseline odometry. This shows that the absolute yaw estimation is still better for the baseline odometry.

    The complex trajectory flat floor experiments show mixed results. For the figure-\texttt{8} and square motion datasets, the baseline odometry performs better than the \gls{cvo} approach in terms of yaw \gls{rmse}, travel distance error, and rotational \gls{rpe} drift. The \gls{cvo} trajectory follows the general shape of the motion, but it shows larger yaw errors and underestimates the travel distance. In the random exploration dataset, the \gls{cvo} approach performs better in terms of yaw \gls{rmse}. This shows that the \gls{cvo} approach can improve some metrics in irregular trajectories, but the improvement is not consistent across all complex trajectory datasets.

    Overall, the flat floor terrain experiments show that the \gls{cvo} approach does not consistently outperform the baseline odometry. The \gls{cvo} approach shows some improvement in local rotational estimation during circular motion and yaw estimation in the random exploration dataset. However, the baseline odometry performs better in several cases, especially in travel distance error, translational \gls{rpe} drift, and yaw estimation. This shows that the proposed approach has some useful improvements, but its performance on flat floor terrain is still not consistent.
       
    \section{Sand Terrain} \label{sec:sandTerrainResults}

    \subsection{Straight-Line Motion}
    
    The datasets collected in the sand terrain experiments with straight-line motion are discussed in this subsection. The rover is commanded to move in a straight line. Unlike, the flat floor terrain, the variations of straight line datasets is limited. The sand terrain straight-line datasets only include move forward pause and move backward motion type. This motion type is performed in three different maximum commanded velocity settings: $0.2~\mathrm{m/s}$, $0.4~\mathrm{m/s}$, and $0.6~\mathrm{m/s}$. The variation in velocity setting is not done in the flat-floor terrain experiments because the high impact force from the high-speed motion can damage the rover systems. In sand terrain, the impact force is lower due to the soft nature of the terrain, which allows for higher speed motion without damaging the rover systems.
    
    The straight-line motion datasets in the sand terrain experiments are: \texttt{DATA\_18\_YY}, \texttt{DATA\_19\_YY} and \texttt{DATA\_20\_YY}. The evaluation results of datasets with the straight line motion type and various velocity settings are presented in the following subsections.

    \subsection{Evaluation Summary of Sand Terrain Experiments}
    
    The straight-line sand terrain experiments show that the performance of the baseline odometry and the \gls{cvo} approach changes depending on the dataset. In some datasets, the baseline odometry performs better in terms of travel distance error and final position error. In other datasets, the \gls{cvo} approach shows better translational \gls{rpe} drift. This shows that the sand terrain introduces more variation in the odometry performance compared to the flat floor terrain.

    The \texttt{z}-position estimation on sand terrain is not consistently improved by the \gls{cvo} approach. In some datasets, the \gls{cvo} approach follows the changes in the \texttt{z}-position better, but in other datasets it drifts away from the ground truth. This shows that the \texttt{z}-position estimation remains challenging on sand terrain.

    The complex sand terrain experiments show mixed results. In \texttt{DATA\_27\_01}, the baseline odometry performs better in terms of global trajectory, final position error, yaw estimation, and \texttt{z}-position estimation. However, the \gls{cvo} approach performs better in terms of translational and rotational \gls{rpe} drift. In \texttt{DATA\_28\_01}, the \gls{cvo} approach performs better in terms of final position error, yaw estimation, and translational \gls{rpe} drift, while the baseline odometry performs better in terms of travel distance error and \texttt{z}-position estimation.

    Overall, the sand terrain experiments show that the \gls{cvo} approach does not consistently outperform the baseline odometry in all metrics. However, the \gls{cvo} approach shows improvements in some datasets, especially in local relative motion estimation and yaw estimation. The baseline odometry still performs better in several cases, especially in travel distance error and \texttt{z}-position estimation. This shows that the proposed approach has useful improvements, but its performance on sand terrain is still not consistent.

The shape of the ground truth trajectory is not followed by the baseline odometry trajectory. This dataset also includes a slip event. This can be seen from the \texttt{z}-position comparison plot shown in Figure~\ref{fig:crater_terrain_dataset_26_04_z_position_slip_comparison}. Between $43~\mathrm{s}$ and $48~\mathrm{s}$, the change in ground truth \texttt{z}-position from $0.3~\mathrm{m}$ to $0.5~\mathrm{m}$, a $0.2~\mathrm{m}$ increase, but the change in the baseline odometry \texttt{z}-position is from $0.9~\mathrm{m}$ to $1.5~\mathrm{m}$, a $0.6~\mathrm{m}$ increase. The \gls{cvo} estimation with and without slip elimination shows similar increase in the \texttt{z}-position from $0.65~\mathrm{m}$ to 
    $1.1~\mathrm{m}$, a $0.45~\mathrm{m}$ increase. Even though the slip is not detected, the \gls{cvo} estimation is closer to the ground truth than the baseline odometry. 
    
    The reason behind this is that, the slip detection works better when all four wheels are slipping. This was the case in the previous dataset where the wheel were rotating, but the rover's position did not change. On the other hand, in this dataset, only the left side wheels were slipping. This could explain the lack performance improvement from the slip elimination. Nevertheless, based on the metrics from Table~\ref{tab:odometry_metrics_data-26-04_beforeAfter_slip_elimination}, the \gls{cvo} estimation with slip elimination shows slightly better performance than the \gls{cvo} estimation without slip elimination and mixed performance compared to the baseline odometry. 
    
    The travel distance error is reduced from $-0.88~\mathrm{m}$ to $-0.85~\mathrm{m}$, the yaw \gls{rmse} is reduced from $5.68~^\circ$ to $5.54~^\circ$, and the \texttt{z}-position \gls{rmse} is reduced from $0.45~\mathrm{m}$ to $0.42~\mathrm{m}$. The translational \gls{rpe} drift \gls{rmse} is the same for both \gls{cvo} estimations, but the rotational \gls{rpe} drift \gls{rmse} is slightly higher for the \gls{cvo} estimation with slip elimination. Overall, the \gls{cvo} estimation with slip elimination shows better performance than the \gls{cvo} estimation without slip elimination. 

    The \gls{cvo} estimation with slip elimination performs better than the baseline odometry in terms of final position error, \texttt{z}-position \gls{rmse}, and the translational \gls{rpe} drift \gls{rmse}. In terms of yaw \gls{rmse} and rotational \gls{rpe} drift \gls{rmse} and travel distance error, the \gls{cvo} estimation with slip elimination performs worse than the baseline odometry. 
    
    \subsection{Evaluation Summary of Crater Terrain Experiments} \label{subsec:craterTerrainEvalSummary}

    The crater terrain experiments show that the \gls{cvo} approach with slip elimination performs better than the \gls{cvo} approach without slip elimination in both selected datasets. This shows that removing slipping wheel measurements is useful in the crater terrain, where the rover moves on an inclined surface and slip is more likely to occur.

    The \gls{cvo} approach with slip elimination performs better than the baseline odometry in terms of final position error, \texttt{z}-position \gls{rmse}, and translational \gls{rpe} drift. This shows that the proposed slip elimination method helps to reduce the effect of slipping wheel measurements in the position estimation.

    However, the baseline odometry performs better in terms of travel distance error, yaw \gls{rmse}, and rotational \gls{rpe} drift. This shows that the \gls{cvo} approach still has limitations in estimating the yaw and travel distance on crater terrain.

    Overall, the crater terrain experiments show that the \gls{cvo} approach with slip elimination gives better results than the \gls{cvo} approach without slip elimination. The proposed approach also improves some metrics compared to the baseline odometry, especially final position error, \texttt{z}-position estimation, and translational \gls{rpe} drift. However, the performance is still not better than the baseline odometry in all metrics.
