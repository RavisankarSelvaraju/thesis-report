    \chapter{Results and Discussion} \label{chap:resultsanddiscussion}

    The evaluation of the proposed method using the experiments and evaluation metrics discussed in the previous chapter is presented in this chapter. The performance comparison of the proposed \gls{cvo} approach and the baseline odometry against the Vicon-based ground truth is discussed. This chapter is organized based on the terrain types used in the experiments. The final part of this chapter presents the discussion of the results obtained from the experiments. 
    
    The flat floor and the sand terrain experiments compare the performance of the baseline odometry and the proposed \gls{cvo} approach only focusing on the \gls{cvo} estimates. The slip elimination part is not discussed in these terrains. The crater terrain experiments explicitly discuss the slip event and the comparison of \gls{cvo} approach with and without slip elimination. 
    
    \section{Flat Floor Terrain Experiments} \label{sec:flatFloorTerrainResults}   
    
    The evaluation results from the datasets presented in Section~\ref{subsec:exp_flatFloorTerrain} are discussed in this section. A total of 53 datasets were collected on flat floor terrain experiments. Since presenting all datasets would make the chapter repetitive, a set of representative datasets is selected for detailed discussion. The presented plots could be separated into two types: the comparison plots and the error plots. The comparison plots the actual values whereas the error plots show the difference between the estimated values and the ground truth values. The comparison plots include X--Y trajectory plots, \texttt{z}-position plots, and yaw orientation plots. The error plots include \texttt{z}-position error plots, yaw orientation error plots, translational and rotational \gls{rpe} plots, and translational and rotational \gls{ape} plots. The evaluation metrics are summarized in tables for each dataset.
    
    The representative datasets are selected to include different trajectory types, such as straight-line motion, circular motion, and complex trajectories. This selection helps to discuss the performance of the proposed method on simple and complex trajectories. Unless stated otherwise, the same color coding is used across all plots. The ground truth trajectory is shown in blue, the baseline odometry trajectory is shown in green, and the contact velocity odometry trajectory is shown in red. The yaw orientations are shown as heading arrows along the trajectories using the same respective colors.
    
    \subsection{Straight-Line Motion}
    
    The straight-line motion datasets include the experiments where the rover moves in a straight-line without any turns. Three straight-line motion types are included on flat floor terrain experiments. The straight-line motion datasets are: \texttt{DATA\_02\_YY}, \texttt{DATA\_03\_YY}, and \texttt{DATA\_04\_YY}, where \texttt{YY} represents the iteration number. The evaluation results of these datasets are presented in the following subsections. 
    
    Since the results from different straight line trajectories explains the similar trends and observations, the evaluation results of the straight-line motion dataset \texttt{DATA\_02\_03} and \texttt{DATA\_04\_02} are presented in this chapter. The evaluation results of the straight-line motion dataset \texttt{DATA\_03\_01} are presented in Appendix Section~\ref{subsubsec:straightLineDD2_03_01}.

The improvement in the yaw orientation estimation of the \gls{cvo} approach in this dataset reduced the final position error. However, the travel distance underestimation is consistent with the previous dataset.

The z-position estimation is worse for circular trajectory compared to the straight line trajectories.

    \subsection{Evaluation Summary of Flat Floor Terrain Experiments} \label{subsec:flatFloorEvalSummary}
    
   The straight-line flat floor experiments show that the baseline odometry performs better than the \gls{cvo} approach in terms of travel distance error and translational \gls{rpe} drift. This shows that the baseline approach is sufficient for simple low-speed straight-line motion on flat floor terrain. Even though the \gls{cvo} approach captures the variation pattern of the \texttt{z}-position better in some cases, the overall performance is not better than the baseline odometry. Specially in complex trajectories, the \gls{cvo} approach does not provide any significant improvement in \texttt{z}-position estimation. 

    The circular motion flat floor experiments show that the rotational \gls{rpe} drift is slightly better for the \gls{cvo} approach compared to the baseline odometry. This shows that the \gls{cvo} approach can provide better local rotational estimates in circular motion. However, the yaw \gls{rmse} is still lower for the baseline odometry. This shows that the absolute yaw estimation is still better for the baseline odometry.

    The complex trajectory flat floor experiments show mixed results. For the figure-\texttt{8} and square motion datasets, the baseline odometry performs better than the \gls{cvo} approach in terms of yaw \gls{rmse}, travel distance error, and rotational \gls{rpe} drift. The \gls{cvo} trajectory follows the general shape of the motion, but it shows larger yaw errors and underestimates the travel distance. In the random exploration dataset, the \gls{cvo} approach performs better in terms of yaw \gls{rmse}. This shows that the \gls{cvo} approach can improve some metrics in irregular trajectories, but the improvement is not consistent across all complex trajectory datasets.

    Overall, the flat floor terrain experiments show that the \gls{cvo} approach does not consistently outperform the baseline odometry. The \gls{cvo} approach shows some improvement in local rotational estimation during circular motion and yaw estimation in the random exploration dataset. However, the baseline odometry performs better in several cases, especially in travel distance error, translational \gls{rpe} drift, and yaw estimation. This shows that the proposed approach has some useful improvements, but its performance on flat floor terrain is still not consistent.

    \section{Sand Terrain} \label{sec:sandTerrainResults}   
    
    The evaluation results of the sand terrain experiments presented in Section~\ref{subsec:exp_sandTerrain} are discussed in this section. A total of 26 datasets were collected on the sand terrain experiments. This section only presents the evaluation results of a representative subset of these datasets. Each dataset provides plots that present the X--Y trajectory comparison, \texttt{z}-position comparison and a summary of key evaluation metrics.     \subsection{Straight-Line Motion}
    
    The datasets collected in the sand terrain experiments with straight-line motion are discussed in this subsection. The rover is commanded to move in a straight line. Unlike, the flat floor terrain, the variations of straight line datasets is limited. The sand terrain straight-line datasets only include move forward pause and move backward motion type. This motion type is performed in three different maximum commanded velocity settings: $0.2~\mathrm{m/s}$, $0.4~\mathrm{m/s}$, and $0.6~\mathrm{m/s}$. The variation in velocity setting is not done in the flat-floor terrain experiments because the high impact force from the high-speed motion can damage the rover systems. In sand terrain, the impact force is lower due to the soft nature of the terrain, which allows for higher speed motion without damaging the rover systems.
    
    The straight-line motion datasets in the sand terrain experiments are: \texttt{DATA\_18\_YY}, \texttt{DATA\_19\_YY} and \texttt{DATA\_20\_YY}. The evaluation results of datasets with the straight line motion type and various velocity settings are presented in the following subsections. Since the results from different straight line trajectories explains the similar trends and observations, the evaluation results of the straight-line motion dataset \texttt{DATA\_18\_02} and \texttt{DATA\_20\_03} are presented in this chapter. The evaluation results of the straight-line motion dataset \texttt{DATA\_19\_02} are presented in Appendix Section~\ref{subsubsec:straightLineMotionD2_19_02}.

    \subsection{Evaluation Summary of Sand Terrain Experiments}
    
    The straight-line sand terrain experiments show that the performance of the baseline odometry and the \gls{cvo} approach changes depending on the dataset. In some datasets, the baseline odometry performs better in terms of travel distance error and final position error. In other datasets, the \gls{cvo} approach shows better translational \gls{rpe} drift. This shows that the sand terrain introduces more variation in the odometry performance compared to the flat floor terrain.

    The \texttt{z}-position estimation on sand terrain is not consistently improved by the \gls{cvo} approach. In some datasets, the \gls{cvo} approach follows the changes in the \texttt{z}-position better, but in other datasets it drifts away from the ground truth. This shows that the \texttt{z}-position estimation remains challenging on sand terrain.

    The complex sand terrain experiments show mixed results. In \texttt{DATA\_27\_01}, the baseline odometry performs better in terms of global trajectory, final position error, yaw estimation, and \texttt{z}-position estimation. However, the \gls{cvo} approach performs better in terms of translational and rotational \gls{rpe} drift. In \texttt{DATA\_28\_01}, the \gls{cvo} approach performs better in terms of final position error, yaw estimation, and translational \gls{rpe} drift, while the baseline odometry performs better in terms of travel distance error and \texttt{z}-position estimation.

    Overall, the sand terrain experiments show that the \gls{cvo} approach does not consistently outperform the baseline odometry in all metrics. However, the \gls{cvo} approach shows improvements in some datasets, especially in local relative motion estimation and yaw estimation. The baseline odometry still performs better in several cases, especially in travel distance error and \texttt{z}-position estimation. This shows that the proposed approach has useful improvements, but its performance on sand terrain is still not consistent. The baseline odometry trajectory shows a significant deviation in the \texttt{y}-position from the ground truth trajectory. This can be explained as the slipping of the wheels when the rover moves up the crater terrain is accounted as the rover motion by the baseline odometry. On the other hand, the \gls{cvo} trajectory when slip elimination is disabled, deviates in the \texttt{x}-position. This deviation is the result of using slipping wheel spokes to update the filter state. This shows that the \gls{cvo} approach when slip elimination is disabled is not able to handle the wheel slip event. The \gls{cvo} trajectory after enabling slip elimination is closer to the ground truth trajectory. With slip elimination enabled, the \gls{cvo} approach rejects inconsistent contact velocity measurements and prevents them from being used in the filter update. This improves the error in both \texttt{x}-position and \texttt{y}-position.

This shows that the \gls{cvo} approach with slip elimination is able to provide a better yaw estimation when the wheel slip event occurs. The yaw error comparison can be seen in Figure~\ref{fig:crater_terrain_dataset_26_01_yaw_error_slip_comparison}.

The translational \gls{rpe} drift \gls{rmse} is reduced from $0.39~\mathrm{m/m}$ to $0.23~\mathrm{m/m}$ after enabling slip elimination. The translational \gls{rpe} drift \gls{rmse} of the \gls{cvo} with slip elimination is lower than the baseline odometry. 
    
    The final position error is also slightly improved from $0.76~\mathrm{m}$ to $0.74~\mathrm{m}$ after enabling slip elimination. The \gls{cvo} with and without slip elimination shows better final position error than the baseline odometry. The absolute travel distance error without slip elimination is $0.39~\mathrm{m}$, which is larger than the baseline odometry travel distance error of $0.31~\mathrm{m}$. After enabling slip elimination, the travel distance error is reduced to $-0.15~\mathrm{m}$, which is a significant improvement from both the baseline odometry and \gls{cvo} without slip elimination. The travel distance error after slip elimination is negative, which indicates that the \gls{cvo} with slip elimination slightly underestimates the travel distance. This is consistent with the previous experiments in other terrains, where the \gls{cvo} approach tends to underestimate the travel distance.

The \texttt{z}-position \gls{rmse} is reduced from $0.26~\mathrm{m}$ to $0.23~\mathrm{m}$ after enabling slip elimination.     \subsection{Evaluation Summary of Crater Terrain Experiments} \label{subsec:craterTerrainEvalSummary}

    \section{Overall Result Interpretation}

        The translational and rotational \gls{rpe} drift \gls{rmse} metrics were selected to compare the performance of the proposed \gls{cvo} approach with the baseline odometry across the presented datasets. These metrics characterize local relative-motion estimation and, although they are still influenced by the motion type and evaluation interval, they are less affected by trajectory duration and accumulated global drift than absolute or endpoint error metrics. The evaluation results show that the performance of the proposed \gls{cvo} approach depends on the terrain, motion type, and evaluated error metric.

        Across the selected flat-floor datasets, the baseline odometry generally provides lower translational and rotational \gls{rpe} drift than the proposed \gls{cvo} approach. The \gls{cvo} approach produces a lower translational \gls{rpe} drift in only one of the nine selected flat-floor datasets and the same value as the baseline in one other dataset. The rotational \gls{rpe} drift is lower for the \gls{cvo} approach in two datasets but higher in the remaining seven datasets. These results show that the \gls{pcv}-based update does not provide a consistent advantage for local motion estimation in the selected flat-floor experiments, where the simpler baseline wheel model generally provides sufficient performance.

        A different trend is observed on sand terrain. The \gls{cvo} approach produces a lower translational \gls{rpe} drift in six of the seven selected sand datasets, indicating that the \gls{pcv}-based update can improve local translational motion estimation on deformable terrain. However, the rotational \gls{rpe} drift is higher for the \gls{cvo} approach in six of the seven selected sand datasets, showing that local rotational estimation remains a weakness of the proposed approach.

        In the crater experiments, enabling slip elimination substantially reduces both the translational and rotational \gls{rpe} drift in \texttt{DATA\_26\_01}, whereas it produces little change in \texttt{DATA\_26\_04}. Compared with the baseline odometry, the \gls{cvo} approach with slip elimination provides lower translational \gls{rpe} drift in both selected crater datasets but higher rotational \gls{rpe} drift. Overall, the results indicate that the proposed approach is most beneficial for local translational motion estimation on deformable terrain and under pronounced slip conditions. However, consistent improvement in rotational estimation and reliable performance across all terrains and motion types were not achieved.
        
        Since, the ground truth data to evaluate the contact estimation using \gls{md} method is not available, the contact estimation results are not discussed in this chapter. However, a sample comparison between the \gls{lf} method and \gls{md} method is presented in Appendix Section~\ref{sec:contactEstimationComparison}.
