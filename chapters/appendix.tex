    \chapter{Additional Implementation Details}
    
    This chapter provides additional implementation details of the \gls{cvo} approach. It includes the derivation of the measurement Jacobian, the numerical calculation of the Jacobian, and a comparison of contact estimation methods. 
    
    \section{Measurement Jacobian Derivation} \label{appendix:MeasurementJacobianDerivation}
    
    This section provides the derivation of the measurement Jacobian presented in Section~\ref{sec:measurementModelAndJacobian}. The Jacobian is derived by calculating the partial derivatives of the measurement function $h(x)$ with respect to each error-state variable. This analytical derivation of the Jacobian matrix was assisted by AI using the Gemini CLI~\cite{geminicli2026}. The equations presented in the following sections were generated using the AI assistant ChatGPT~\cite{openai_chatgpt_jacobian_2026} and manually verified for correctness. 
    
    \subsection{Rotation Matrix}

    The rotation matrix block of the measurement Jacobian is derived from the measurement function $h(x)$, which is defined as follows:

    \begin{equation}
    h(x) =
    -\left(
    [R_{body}^{odom}]^{T}v_{body}^{odom}
    +
    \left(\omega_{body}^{body}\times r_{pc}^{body}\right)
    \right)
    \end{equation}

    The frame notations for the quantities in the measurement function do not change in this derivation, so the frame names are dropped for compactness. The $\widehat{\phantom{x}}$ represents that the quantity is part of the estimated state $\hat x$.
    For a small rotation error $\xi^R$, the first-order approximation of the body velocity is
        
    \begin{equation}
        [\hat R_{\mathrm{pert}}]^{T}\hat v
        \approx
        [\hat R]^{T}\hat v
        +
        \left[
        [\hat R]^{T}\hat v
        \right]_{\times}\xi^R
    \end{equation}

    Substituting this approximation into the measurement function $h(x)$, we get,

    \begin{align}
        h(\hat x_{\mathrm{pert}}(\xi^R))
        &\approx
        -\left(
        [\hat R]^{T}\hat v + \left[ [\hat R]^{T}\hat v \right]_{\times}\xi^R +
        \left(
        \hat \omega\times r
        \right)
        \right)
        \\
        &=
        -\left(
        [\hat R]^{T}\hat v
        +
        \left(
        \hat \omega\times r
        \right)
        \right)
        -
        \left[
        [\hat R]^{T}\hat v
        \right]_{\times}\xi^R
        \\
        &=
        h(\hat x)
        -
        \left[
        [\hat R]^{T}\hat v
        \right]_{\times}\xi^R.
    \end{align}

    Where, the notation  $x_{\mathrm{pert}}(\xi^R)$ can be defined as 
    \begin{equation}
        \hat x_{\mathrm{pert}}(\xi^R)
        =
        \left(
            \hat{X}
            \operatorname{Exp}
            \left(
                \begin{bmatrix}
                    \xi^R \\
                    \mathbf{0} \\
                    \mathbf{0}
                \end{bmatrix}
            \right),
            \hat{b}
        \right).
        \label{eq:rotationPerturbedState}
    \end{equation}

    A small rotation error $\xi^R$ perturbs the estimated state $\hat x$. Therefore, the partial derivative of the measurement function with respect to the rotation error is
    
    \begin{equation}
    \frac{\partial h(\hat x_{\mathrm{pert}}(\xi^R))}
    {\partial \xi^R}
    \bigg|_{\xi^R=0}
    =
    -\left[
    [\hat R]^{T}\hat v
    \right]_{\times}.
    \end{equation} 
    
    \subsection{Velocity}
    A small velocity error $\xi^v$ changes the measurement function as

    \begin{equation}
        h(\hat x_{\mathrm{pert}}(\xi^v))
        =
        -\left(
        [\hat R]^{T}\hat v
        +
        \xi^v
        +
        \left(
        \hat \omega\times r
        \right)
        \right).
        \label{eq:velocityperturb}
    \end{equation}
   
    Where, the notation $x_{\mathrm{pert}}(\xi^v)$ can be defined as 
    \begin{equation}
        \hat x_{\mathrm{pert}}(\xi^v)
        =
        \left(
            \hat{X}
            \operatorname{Exp}
            \left(
                \begin{bmatrix}
                    \mathbf{0} \\
                    \xi^v \\
                    \mathbf{0}
                \end{bmatrix}
            \right),
            \hat{b}
        \right).
        \label{eq:velocityPerturbedState}
    \end{equation}

    Using the definition of $h(x)$, Equation~\ref{eq:velocityperturb} can be written as

    \begin{equation}
        h(\hat x_{\mathrm{pert}}(\xi^v))
        =
        h(\hat x)-\xi^v.
    \end{equation}

    Differentiating with respect to $\xi^v$ gives

    \begin{equation}
        \frac{\partial h(x_{\mathrm{pert}}(\xi^v))}
        {\partial \xi^v}\bigg|_{\xi^v=0}
        =
        -\mathbf{I}.
    \end{equation}

    \subsection{Position}

    The body position does not appear in the measurement function. Therefore, adding a small position error $\xi^p$ does not change the predicted measurement:

    \begin{equation}
        h(\hat x_{\mathrm{pert}}(\xi^p))=h(\hat x).
    \end{equation}

    Where, the notation $\hat x_{\mathrm{pert}}(\xi^p)$ can be defined as 
    \begin{equation}
        \hat x_{\mathrm{pert}}(\xi^p)
        =
        \left(
            \hat{X}
            \operatorname{Exp}
            \left(
                \begin{bmatrix}
                    \mathbf{0} \\
                    \mathbf{0} \\
                    \xi^p
                \end{bmatrix}
            \right),
            \hat{b}
        \right).
        \label{eq:positionPerturbedState}
    \end{equation}

    Differentiating with respect to $\xi^p$ gives

    \begin{equation}
        \frac{\partial h(\hat x_{\mathrm{pert}}(\xi^p))}
        {\partial \xi^p}\bigg|_{\xi^p=0}
        =
        \mathbf{0}.
    \end{equation}

    \subsection{Gyroscope Bias}

    The gyroscope bias is included in the bias-corrected angular velocity:

    \begin{equation}
        \hat \omega
        =
        \omega_{meas}^{body}-b_g.
    \end{equation}

    After applying a small gyroscope-bias error $\zeta^{b_g}$, the measurement function becomes

\begin{align}
    h\left(\hat x_{\mathrm{pert}}(\zeta^{b_g})\right)
    &=
    -\left(
        \hat{R}^{T}\hat{v}
        +
        \left(
            \omega_{\mathrm{meas}}^{body}
            -
            \left(
                \hat{b}_g+\zeta^{b_g}
            \right)
        \right)
        \times r
    \right)
    \\
    &=
    -\left(
        \hat{R}^{T}\hat{v}
        +
        \left(
            \omega_{\mathrm{meas}}^{body}
            -
            \hat{b}_g
            -
            \zeta^{b_g}
        \right)
        \times r
    \right)
    \\
    &=
    h(\hat{x})
    +
    \zeta^{b_g}\times r
    \\
    &=
    h(\hat{x})
    -
    [r]_{\times}\zeta^{b_g}.
\end{align}

    Where, the notation $\hat x_{\mathrm{pert}}(\zeta^{b_g})$ can be defined as 
    
\begin{equation}
    \hat x_{\mathrm{pert}}(\zeta^{b_g})
    =
    \left(
        \hat{X},
        \begin{bmatrix}
            \hat{b}_g+\zeta^{b_g} \\
            \hat{b}_a
        \end{bmatrix}
    \right).
    \label{eq:gyroBiasPerturbedState}
\end{equation}

    Differentiating with respect to $\zeta^{b_g}$ gives

    \begin{equation}
        \frac{\partial h(\hat x_{\mathrm{pert}}(\zeta^{b_g}))}
        {\partial \zeta^{b_g}}\bigg|_{\zeta^{b_g}=0}
        =
        -[r]_{\times}.
    \end{equation}

    \subsection{Accelerometer Bias}

    The accelerometer bias does not appear directly in the measurement function. Therefore, adding a small accelerometer-bias error $\delta b_a$ does not change the predicted measurement:

    \begin{equation}
       h\left(\hat x_{\mathrm{pert}}(\zeta^{b_a})\right)=h(\hat x).
    \end{equation}

Where, the notation $\hat x_{\mathrm{pert}}(\zeta^{b_a})$ can be defined as 
    
\begin{equation}
    \hat x_{\mathrm{pert}}(\zeta^{b_a})
    =
    \left(
        \hat{X},
        \begin{bmatrix}
            \hat{b}_g \\
            \hat{b}_a +\zeta^{b_a}
        \end{bmatrix}
    \right).
    \label{eq:accBiasPerturbedState}
\end{equation}

    Differentiating with respect to $\delta b_a$ gives

    \begin{equation}
        \frac{\partial h(\hat x_{\mathrm{pert}}(\zeta^{b_a}))}
        {\partial \zeta^{b_a}}\bigg|_{\zeta^{b_a}=0}
        =
        \mathbf{0}.
    \end{equation}

    \section{Numerical Jacobian} \label{appendix:NumericalJacobianCalculation}

    The analytical measurement Jacobian derived in Appendix~\ref{appendix:MeasurementJacobianDerivation} is verified using a numerical Jacobian. Each error-state variable is perturbed independently, and the corresponding change in the measurement function is computed using the central-difference approximation. This numerical Jacobian validation implementation and the following equations were developed with the help of AI assistant ChatGPT~\cite{openai_chatgpt_jacobian_2026}. The script is carefully reviewed and verified for correctness. 

    For the $i$-th error-state variable, the corresponding column of the numerical
    Jacobian is calculated as

    \begin{equation}
        H_{\mathrm{num},i}
        =
        \frac{
            h\left(x\boxplus\epsilon_i e_i\right)
            -
            h\left(x\boxplus(-\epsilon_i e_i)\right)
        }{2\epsilon_i},
        \label{eq:numericalJacobianCentralDifference}
    \end{equation}

    where, $e_i$ is the unit vector corresponding to the $i$-th error-state variable, $\epsilon_i$ is the applied perturbation, and $\boxplus$ represents the addition of the perturbation to the state. The rotation perturbation is applied on $SO(3)$ using

    \begin{equation}
        R^{+}
        =
        R\exp\left([\delta R]_{\times}\right),
    \end{equation}

    while the velocity, position, and bias perturbations are added directly to their corresponding state variables. For the selected test state and perturbation values, the analytical and numerical Jacobians were consistent. The Python implementation used for this verification is provided in Listing~\ref{lst:numericalJacobianVerification}.

    \section{Contact Estimation Comparison} \label{sec:contactEstimationComparison}
    
    This section provides a contact estimation comparison between the \gls{md} based and \gls{lf} based estimation. This is not provided in the main report because the contact estimation cannot be verified with ground truth. The ground truth can be obtained from the videos collected during the experiments. Due to the lack of time, the videos were not analyzed to get the ground truth. The comparison in this section uses the \gls{lf} method as a pseudo-ground truth. The \gls{lf} method is used as the reference because its geometric assumption is expected to be more reliable on flat solid terrain. 
    
    The goal of this section is to show that the contact estimation problem has been worked on and evaluate the level of agreement between the \gls{md} based and \gls{lf} based contact estimates. Since the filter state is corrected using the \gls{lf} selected contacts during this comparison, the resulting \gls{md} estimates are not independent of the \gls{lf} method.
    
    For the comparison, the contact estimation is evaluated for the dataset \texttt{DATA\_04\_02}. Figure~\ref{fig:contactEstimationComparison} shows the contact estimates for each leg of the Coyote~3 rover over time. The $X$ marks the contact selected by the \gls{md} method and the $O$ marks the contact selected by the \gls{lf} method. It can be observed from the plot that, when the rover is stationary, the \gls{md} method is uncertain on which spoke is in contact. When the rover is moving, the \gls{md} method can select the same spoke as the \gls{lf} method. The contact estimation metrics are shown in Figure~\ref{fig:contactEstimationComparisonLegend} and Table~\ref{tab:contact_selection_metrics}. The metrics are calculated over the entire dataset. Precision measures the proportion of MD-selected contacts that are also selected by the LF method. Recall measures the proportion of LF-selected contacts recovered by the MD method. The Jaccard index measures the overlap between the complete LF and MD contact sets, while the exact-match rate measures the percentage of synchronized wheel samples for which both methods select identical contact sets.
