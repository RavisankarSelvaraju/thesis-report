    \chapter{Experiments and Evaluation} \label{chap:experimentandevaluation}
        
    The proposed \gls{cvo} approach is evaluated on the Coyote~3 \gls{rwr} through various experiments across diverse terrain and trajectory conditions. The \gls{cvo} estimates are compared with the ground-truth pose estimates from the Vicon motion capture system. The \gls{cvo} estimates are also compared against the baseline odometry estimates to evaluate the difference in performance between the baseline odometry and the proposed \gls{cvo}. The \gls{cvo} approach is implemented in the Coyote~3 rover, and the data required to run the \gls{cvoe} component are collected using the ROCK data-logging components. The main logged data are the \gls{imu} and the joint encoder data. The Vicon pose estimates are also logged and used as ground truth in evaluation. Multiple experiments are conducted, and the mentioned data are logged. The evaluation focuses on the error between the estimated trajectory from the \gls{cvo} and the ground-truth trajectory derived from the Vicon pose estimates. The evaluation uses metrics such as \gls{ape}, \gls{rpe}, translational and rotational drift rates, and statistical error values including minimum, maximum, mean, and standard deviation. 
    
    This chapter describes the Coyote~3 platform used for the experiments, the experimental site and terrain conditions, the datasets with trajectory types, the evaluation metrics, and the evaluation results of the proposed \gls{cvo} approach against the ground truth and baseline odometry estimates.
        
    \section{Coyote~3 Rover Platform}
    
    The Coyote~3 \gls{rwr} platform is used to evaluate the proposed \gls{cvo} approach. This section describes the rover configuration, the sensors equipped on the rover, the control method, and the onboard computation module used during the experiments. The Coyote~3 rover is equipped with four \glspl{rw} attached to four $48 V$ \texttt{RoboDrive ILM 50x08} BLDC motors combined with \texttt{HarmonicDrive CPG 17-80} gearboxes. The front wheels are attached to the main rover body, and the rear wheel axle is attached to the main body through a passive bogie joint. All wheel joints, along with the bogie joint, are equipped with \texttt{iC-Haus MU} absolute encoders. Two RGB-D cameras are mounted on the front and rear of the rover to get the depth images. A Velodyne LiDAR mounted at the front of the rover is used to obtain 3D point cloud data. The camera and LiDAR sensors are used to collect RGB images and depth maps for specific datasets, which can later be used for visual odometry and SLAM. These additional data are not used to evaluate the proposed approach in this thesis.
    
    \begin{figure}[ht]
        \centering
        \includegraphics[width=0.6\linewidth]{media/images/coyote3-component-labelled.jpg}
        \caption{Coyote~3 rover with components annotated.}
        \label{fig:annotated-coyote3-components}
    \end{figure}
    
    A \texttt{MTi-680(G)} \cite{DT-IMU-datasheet} series IMU is used to measure the linear acceleration and angular velocity of the robot body. The \gls{imu} sensor is located inside the main body of the rover. To track the rover with the Vicon motion capture system, four reflective markers are placed on the rover's body. The Coyote~3 rover is equipped with an onboard computer with \texttt{Intel Core i7-3555LE @ 2.5 GHz} processor. The rover is controlled using a gamepad and powered using $2 \times 4500$~mAh LiPo batteries. The rimless wheels are equipped with rubber feet to improve friction with the ground and reduce impact forces during foot-ground contact. The Coyote~3 system lacks a suspension, so the forces on the wheels during the contact phase are directly sensed by the IMU. The rubber foot smooths this effect. The components described in this section are annotated in Figure~\ref{fig:annotated-coyote3-components}.

    \section{Experiment Site and Terrain Conditions} \label{sec:experiment-site-terrain}
    
    The experiments are conducted in the indoor experiment area at \gls{ric}, \gls{dfki}, Bremen, Germany. This experimental area is called the Multifunctional Hall. This hall is mainly divided into two sections: the flexible experimental area and the crater landscape. Within the crater hall, "The area in front of the crater is filled with fine-grained granulate\footnote{This line is taken from the \gls{dfki} website \cite{SITE-dfki-mfh}}", which is treated as the sand terrain in this thesis.  Detailed information on the experimental area can be found on the \gls{dfki} website \cite{SITE-dfki-mfh}. In this report, the experimental area is classified into three terrain conditions: flat floor, sand, and crater. Since all three locations are indoors, the GPS/GNSS data cannot be used to estimate ground-truth pose. To measure the pose of systems during the experiments, the Multifunctional Hall is equipped with 21 Vicon motion-tracking cameras. For the thesis approach evaluation, 83 sets of experimental data were collected in the Multifunctional Hall. The description of each terrain and the number of experiments conducted in each terrain are described in the following sections.

    \section{Evaluation Metrics and Methods}
    
    Comparing a trajectory estimated by a method under evaluation with a ground-truth trajectory is a common practice in robotics~\cite{zhangEvaluationMetrics2018}. In this thesis, the method under evaluation is the proposed \gls{cvo} approach, and the ground-truth trajectory is obtained from the Vicon motion-capture system. The baseline odometry is also compared with the Vicon trajectory, and the resulting error metrics are compared with the metrics obtained from the \gls{cvo} evaluation. This section presents the evaluation metrics used in these comparisons. Each evaluation metric represents a different aspect of odometry estimation, as discussed in the following sections.
    
    \subsection{Trajectory Preprocessing and Comparisons}
    
    For the evaluation, two main comparisons are considered. The first comparison is between the \gls{cvo}- estimated trajectory and the ground-truth trajectory. The second comparison is between the baseline odometry trajectory and the ground truth trajectory. The evaluation metrics are calculated for both comparisons. The evaluation is performed for a set of selected trajectories that are presented in Section~\ref{sec:experiment-site-terrain}. The ground truth pose estimates are recorded at a frequency of approximately $75$~Hz and the pose estimates from the \gls{cvoe} component are also logged at the same frequency. Even though the pose estimate samples are recorded at a similar frequency, both data streams use different timestamps. The \gls{cvoe} components retain the timestamps from the \gls{imu} samples. Since the \gls{imu} and Vicon data streams are recorded during the same experiment, the timestamps can be aligned without any fixed offset. To compare the two trajectories, the estimated and ground-truth timestamps are aligned. The data samples from the estimated trajectory are paired with the closest timestamp in the ground-truth trajectory. Samples with the closest timestamp outside the allowed time difference are discarded. After the alignment process, the estimated and ground-truth data streams have the same number of samples and the same timestamps. 
        
    Before comparing the trajectories, the estimated trajectory's initial pose is aligned with the ground truth trajectory's initial pose. The Vicon pose estimates use a fixed reference frame that differs from the odometry reference frame ($odom$), which is created at the rover's starting location. The trajectories are aligned by computing the transformation from the estimated trajectory's initial pose to the ground truth trajectory's initial pose. The calculated transformation is applied to all the poses of the estimated trajectory.   
    
    \subsection{Absolute Pose Error} 
        
    The \gls{ape} is computed by calculating the difference between a pose in the ground truth trajectory at sample index $i$ and the corresponding pose in the estimated trajectory. Let a pose in the ground truth trajectory at index \texttt{i} be defined as $\mathbf{T}^{gt}_i$ and a pose in the estimated trajectory at the same index be defined as $\mathbf{T}^{est}_i$. These poses are presented in Equation~\ref{eq:EstAndGtPose}. 

    \begin{equation}
            \mathbf{T}^{gt}_i =
            \begin{bmatrix}
                \mathbf{R}^{gt}_i & \mathbf{p}^{gt}_i \\
                \mathbf{0}^\top & 1
            \end{bmatrix}, \hspace{0.5cm}
            \mathbf{T}^{est}_i =
            \begin{bmatrix}
                \mathbf{R}^{est}_i & \mathbf{p}^{est}_i \\
                \mathbf{0}^\top & 1
            \end{bmatrix}
    \label{eq:EstAndGtPose}
    \end{equation}
    
    The number of samples is the same in both data streams after the alignment process, as discussed before. The translational error between $\mathbf{T}^{est}_i$ and $\mathbf{T}^{gt}_i$ is calculated as the Euclidean distance between the position vectors of the two poses as shown in Equation~\ref{eq:ape_translational_error}. The rotational error is calculated as the geodesic angle between the two orientation quaternions as shown in Equation~\ref{eq:ape_rotational_error}.

    \begin{equation}
        \begin{aligned}
            \mathbf{p}_{i}^{err}
            &=
            \mathbf{p}^{est}_i - \mathbf{p}^{gt}_i,
            &
            e^{trans}_{i}
            &=
            \left\|
                \mathbf{p}_{i}^{err}
            \right\|
        \end{aligned}
        \label{eq:ape_translational_error}
    \end{equation}

    \begin{equation}
        \begin{aligned}
            \mathbf{q}_{i}^{err}
            &=
            \left(\mathbf{q}^{gt}_i\right)^{-1}
            \otimes
            \mathbf{q}^{est}_i,
            &
            e^{rot}_{i}
            &=
            \left\|
                \log
                \left(
                    \mathbf{q}_{i}^{err}
                \right)
            \right\|
        \end{aligned}
        \label{eq:ape_rotational_error}
    \end{equation}
    
    \begin{figure}[ht!]
        \centering
        \includegraphics[width=0.6\textwidth]{media/plots/APE_explanation_plot_1.png}
        \caption[Absolute Pose Error (APE) explanation plot]{Absolute Pose Error (APE) between estimated and ground truth trajectories. The errors between the corresponding poses are shown as e1, e2, e3, ..., en.}
        \label{fig:ape_explanation_plot_1}  
    \end{figure}  

    Here, $\mathbf{p}_{i}^{err}$ is the translational error vector, $\mathbf{q}_{i}^{err}$ is the orientation error quaternion, $e^{trans}_{i}$ is the scalar Euclidean translational error, and $e^{rot}_{i}$ is the scalar geodesic rotational error. The \gls{ape} error calculation between the corresponding pose pairs is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:ape_explanation_plot_1}. This calculation is repeated for all corresponding pose pairs until the index $i$ reaches the end of the trajectory. The \gls{rmse} of the translational and rotational error is calculated to quantify the complete trajectory error. The \gls{ape} metric is useful for evaluating the global consistency of the odometry because it measures the pose error at each timestamp with respect to the corresponding ground-truth pose in the \texttt{odom} frame.
    
    \subsection{Relative Pose Error} \label{sec:rpeMetrics}
    
    The \gls{rpe} between the ground truth (gt) trajectory and the estimated (est) trajectory can be calculated using the following steps. A distance-based sliding window is created with the initial index \texttt{i} and a new index \texttt{j}, where $j>i$. Cumulative travel distance from the starting pose of the gt and every pose in the gt is calculated and stored. This cumulative distance is used when the sliding window is created. This value is set to $0.5~m$ because the SLAM system updates every $0.5 m$ in translation. Thus, the results in this metric are directly informative about the quality of the solution with respect to its integration with SLAM.

    The Relative Pose Error is computed by comparing two relative poses, each derived independently from estimated and ground-truth trajectories. The first relative pose is obtained by transforming $\mathbf{T}^{est}_j$ into the local frame of $\mathbf{T}^{est}_i$. The second relative pose is obtained similarly by transforming $\mathbf{T}^{gt}_j$ into the local frame of $\mathbf{T}^{gt}_i$. Since $\mathbf{T}^{est}_i$ and $\mathbf{T}^{gt}_i$ do not coincide in the \texttt{odom} frame, each relative pose is expressed in its own local coordinate frame. The relative poses of gt and est are expressed as $\Delta\mathbf{T}^{gt}_{i,j}$ and $\Delta\mathbf{T}^{est}_{i,j}$. The transformation of poses within the sliding window to their local frame and the calculation of \gls{rpe} are shown in Figure~\ref{fig:rpe_explanation_plot}. 
    
    In the implementation of \gls{rpe} metric, the relative poses are calculated as relative position vectors and relative orientation quaternions. The relative position vectors and relative orientation quaternions are calculated using Equations~\ref{eq:rpe_relative_translation} and \ref{eq:rpe_relative_quaternion}. 
        
    \begin{figure}[ht!]
        \centering
        \includegraphics[width=0.6\textwidth]{media/plots/rpe_explanation_plot_1.png}
        \includegraphics[width=0.6\textwidth]{media/plots/rpe_explanation_plot_2.png}
        \caption[Sliding Window based Relative Pose Error (RPE) explanation plot. ]{Sliding Window-based Relative Pose Error (RPE) calculation between estimated and ground truth trajectories. The relative poses are transformed into their local coordinate frames, and the error between them is calculated. }
        \label{fig:rpe_explanation_plot}  
    \end{figure}
    
    
    \begin{equation}
        \begin{aligned}
        \Delta \mathbf{p}^{gt}_{i,j}
        =
        \left(
            \mathbf{R}^{gt}_{i}
        \right)^{\top}
        \left(
            \mathbf{p}^{gt}_{j} - \mathbf{p}^{gt}_{i}
        \right), \hspace{0.5cm}
        \Delta \mathbf{p}^{est}_{i,j}
        =
        \left(
            \mathbf{R}^{est}_{i}
        \right)^{\top}
        \left(
            \mathbf{p}^{est}_{j} - \mathbf{p}^{est}_{i}
        \right)
        \end{aligned}
        \label{eq:rpe_relative_translation}
    \end{equation}

    
    \begin{equation}
        \begin{aligned}
        \Delta \mathbf{q}^{gt}_{i,j}
        =
        \left(
            \mathbf{q}^{gt}_{i}
        \right)^{-1}
        \otimes
        \mathbf{q}^{gt}_{j}, \hspace{0.5cm}
        \Delta \mathbf{q}^{est}_{i,j}
        =
        \left(
            \mathbf{q}^{est}_{i}
        \right)^{-1}
        \otimes
        \mathbf{q}^{est}_{j}
        \end{aligned}
        \label{eq:rpe_relative_quaternion}
    \end{equation}
   
    
    \begin{equation}
        \begin{aligned}
            \mathbf{p}^{err}_{i,j}
            &=
            \Delta \mathbf{p}^{est}_{i,j}
            -
            \Delta \mathbf{p}^{gt}_{i,j},
            &
            e^{trans}_{i,j}
            &=
            \left\|
                \mathbf{p}^{err}_{i,j}
            \right\|
        \end{aligned}
        \label{eq:rpe_translational_error}
    \end{equation}

    \begin{equation}
        \begin{aligned}
            \mathbf{q}^{err}_{i,j}
            &=
            \left(
                \Delta \mathbf{q}^{gt}_{i,j}
            \right)^{-1}
            \otimes
            \Delta \mathbf{q}^{est}_{i,j},
            &
            e^{rot}_{i,j}
            &=
            \left\|
                \log
                \left(
                    \mathbf{q}^{err}_{i,j}
                \right)
            \right\|
        \end{aligned}
        \label{eq:rpe_rotational_error}
    \end{equation}

    The $\Delta \mathbf{p}^{est}_{i,j}$ and $\Delta \mathbf{p}^{gt}_{i,j}$ are the relative translation vectors. The $\Delta \mathbf{q}^{est}_{i,j}$ and $\Delta \mathbf{q}^{gt}_{i,j}$ are the relative orientation quaternions. The $\mathbf{p}^{err}_{i,j}$ and $\mathbf{q}^{err}_{i,j}$ are the \texttt{3D} relative position error vector and relative rotation error quaternion, respectively. These calculations are shown in Equations~\ref{eq:rpe_translational_error} and \ref{eq:rpe_rotational_error}. 
       
    These vector error quantities are converted into scalar error values by taking the Euclidean norm of the translational error vector and the geodesic angle of the rotational error quaternion. The $e^{trans}_{i,j}$ and $e^{rot}_{i,j}$ are the scalar translational and rotational error values respectively. Therefore, each sliding window produces one translational error in meters and one rotational error in radians. The sliding window is then shifted along the trajectory, sample by sample, until a valid sliding window cannot be created given the distance threshold. At the end of the trajectory, two lists of relative translation and rotation error is available. These values are used to calculate \gls{rmse} of translation and rotation error to quantify the error over the complete trajectory. 
    
    The \gls{rpe} metric is specifically useful in odometry evaluation because odometry is based on incremental motion estimation. Since the metric computes the error between two relative poses within the sliding window, the influence of errors accumulated before the pose at index $i$ is reduced. This helps us determine the local consistency of the odometry without the influence of accumulated global errors.
    
    \subsection{Drift Rate}
    
    The Drift Rate is calculated as part of the \gls{rpe} error metric. After the \gls{rmse} values of the translational and rotational \gls{rpe} are calculated as described in Section~\ref{sec:rpeMetrics}, these values are divided by the sliding-window distance threshold to obtain the translational and rotational drift rates. The translational drift rate is calculated as shown in Equation~\ref{eq:translationalDriftRate} and expressed in meters per meter. The rotational drift rate is calculated as shown in Equation~\ref{eq:rotationalDriftRate} and expressed in radians per meter, which is converted to degrees per meter for better interpretation. The drift rate metric is useful to evaluate the odometry performance in terms of the local error accumulated per unit distance travelled by the rover. This helps evaluate the odometry and determine whether it is suitable for SLAM and other navigational tasks. This metric is also useful to compare different odometry methods and their performance.
    
    \begin{equation}
        \text{Translational Drift Rate} = \frac{\text{RMSE of translational RPE (m)}}{\text{Sliding Window Threshold (m)}}
        \label{eq:translationalDriftRate}
    \end{equation}
    
    \begin{equation}
        \text{Rotational Drift Rate} = \frac{\text{RMSE of rotational RPE (radians)}}{\text{Sliding Window Threshold (m)}}
        \label{eq:rotationalDriftRate}
    \end{equation}
       
    \subsection{Statistical Error Values}
    
    Apart from the above-discussed metrics, the statistical error metrics such as minimum, maximum, mean, and standard deviation of the translational and rotational \gls{ape} and \gls{rpe} are also calculated. These statistical values are helpful to understand the error distribution of the odometry estimates. These metrics also helped during debugging the implementation of the \gls{cvo} library.  
    
    The key statistical metric used for the estimated trajectory evaluation is the \gls{rmse} of the translational and rotational errors. This metric quantifies the error over the entire trajectory and compares the performance of the proposed \gls{cvo} approach against the baseline odometry. These statistical metrics are calculated for the baseline odometry and the \gls{cvo}- estimated trajectory relative to the ground-truth trajectory. The comparison of the metrics between the baseline odometry and the \gls{cvo} approach is presented in Section \ref{chap:resultsanddiscussion}. A sample summary of the statistical error metrics is shown in Listing~\ref{lst:statistical-error-metrics}. 
    
    \subsection{Evaluation Plots}
    
    The evaluation metrics are calculated for the collected datasets and the results are visualized using plots. This helps to visualize the performance of the proposed \gls{cvo} approach against the ground-truth trajectory. The evaluation plots are generated using the \texttt{matplotlib} Python library. Key evaluation plots used in this work include the \texttt{x y} trajectory plots with heading vectors, the position comparison between the estimated and ground truth trajectories in $x$, $y$, and $z$ axes, the orientation comparison between the estimated and ground truth trajectories in roll, pitch, and yaw angles, the translational and rotational \gls{ape} plots, and the translational and rotational \gls{rpe} plots. These evaluation plots are used to visualize and compare the performance of the proposed \gls{cvo} approach against the baseline odometry and the ground truth trajectory in the next chapter. These evaluation plots and metrics are integrated into the \texttt{validation toolset}, a library for evaluating the performance of different robotic components. Therefore, several of the developed plots and metrics can be reused to evaluate odometry approaches and other pose estimation algorithms in future work.  
        
    Other evaluation plots are also generated to tune the \gls{cvo} parameters and to debug the implementation of the \gls{cvo} library. These plots include time-colored trajectory plots, Position covariance as an ellipse overlay on the \texttt{x y} trajectory plot, Error histogram of Roll, Pitch, and Yaw angles, Position error plot in $x$, $y$, and $z$ axes, Orientation error plot in roll, pitch, and yaw angles, \gls{imu} acceleration and gyroscope bias evolution over elapsed time and Contact selection comparison between the \gls{lf} and \gls{md} methods. These other plots are not included in this thesis report but are developed and used during the development of the \gls{cvo} library and the evaluation of the proposed approach. 
    
    Some key evaluation metrics, such as the travel distance and final position errors, are also calculated to evaluate the performance of the proposed \gls{cvo} approach. The travel distance is calculated as the sum of the Euclidean distances between consecutive 3D positions in a trajectory. The travel distance error is calculated as the difference between the estimated and ground truth travel distances. A negative travel distance error indicates that the estimated trajectory underestimates the distance travelled by the rover, while a positive value indicates an overestimation. Since the calculation uses the three-dimensional position, changes in the \texttt{z}-position are also included. This is important for evaluating trajectories on inclined and uneven terrain. The final position error is the Euclidean distance between the final estimated position and the final ground truth position. The final position error indicates the accumulated position error at the end of the trajectory.
