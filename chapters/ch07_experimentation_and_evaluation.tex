    \chapter{Experiment and evaluation} \label{chap:experimentandevaluation}
        
    \section{Experiment Objective}
    
    The purpose of the experiments is to evaluate the proposed \gls{cvo} approach in the Coyote~3 \gls{rwr} platform under various terrain and trajectory conditions. The \gls{cvo} estimates are compared against the ground truth pose estimates from the Vicon motion capture system. The \gls{cvo} estimates are also compared against the baseline odometry estimates to evaluate the difference in performance between the baseline odometry and the proposed \gls{cvo}. The \gls{cvo} approach is implemented in the Coyote~3 rover and the data necessary to run the \gls{cvoe} component are collected using the ROCK data logging components. The main logged data are the \gls{imu} and the joint encoder data. The Vicon pose estimates are also logged and used as ground truth in evaluation. Multiple experiments are conducted, and the mentioned data are logged. In addition to the above-mentioned data, the RGB image and the depth map are also logged for some specific datasets so that these datasets can be later used for visual odometry and SLAM. These additional data are not used for the evaluation of the proposed approach in this thesis. The experimental site, terrain and dataset types are described in Section~\ref{sec:experiment-site-terrain}. The evaluation focuses on the error between the estimated trajectory from the \gls{cvo} and the ground truth trajectory from the Vicon pose estimates. The evaluation uses metrics such as \gls{ape}, \gls{rpe}, translational and rotational drift rate, and statistical error values such as minimum, maximum, mean, and standard deviation. 
    
    \section{Experiment Site and Terrain Conditions} \label{sec:experiment-site-terrain}
    
    The experiments are conducted in the indoor experiment area at \gls{ric}, \gls{dfki}, Bremen, Germany. This experiment area is called Multifunctional Hall. This hall is mainly divided into two sections, the flexible experimental area and the crater landscape. Within the crater hall, "The area in front of the crater is filled with fine-grained granulate\footnote{This line is taken from the \gls{dfki} website \cite{SITE-dfki-mfh}}", which is treated as the sand terrain in this thesis.  Detailed information on the experimental area can be found in the \gls{dfki} website \cite{SITE-dfki-mfh}. In this report, the experimental area is classified into three terrain conditions: flat floor terrain, sand terrain, and crater terrain. Since, all three locations are indoors, the GPS/GNSS data cannot be used for ground truth pose estimation. To measure the pose of systems during the experiments, the Multifunctional Hall is covered with 21 Vicon motion tracking cameras. For the thesis approach evaluation, 83 sets of experimental data are collected in the Multifunctional Hall. The description of each terrain and the number of experiments conducted in each terrain is described in the following sections. 
    
    \subsection{Flat Floor Terrain}
      
    The flat experimental area in the Multifunctional Hall is used as the flat floor terrain for the experiments. The flat floor is made of solid concrete. During the experiments, objects are placed in the flat floor area to create a feature rich RGB and depth map image data. The flat floor terrain is shown in Figure \ref{fig:Flat-Floor-Terrain-in-Multifunctional-Hall}. Approximately, 8~m $\times$ 5~m area is chosen as the experiment area within the flat floor terrain. This area is completely covered by the Vicon motion tracking camera to collect the ground truth data.

    The flat floor terrain is used for 19 unique experiments and 17 of those experiments are repeated three times. In total, 53 set of experimental data is available from the flat terrain experiments. The trajectory types used in the flat floor terrain experiments are presented in Table~\ref{tab:flat-terrain-experiments}. The \texttt{Dataset group} name in the table is designed as \texttt{DATA\_XX\_YY} where \texttt{XX} is the unique experiment number and \texttt{YY} is the repetition number. The \texttt{Dataset group} name convention is the same for all the experiments conducted for this thesis. The \texttt{slope} column of the table represents the slope of the terrain in which the experiment is conducted. In the flat floor terrain, the slope is $0^\circ$ for most of the experiments. However, in some experiments involving obstacles, the rover climbs up and down the obstacles in the flat floor terrain which makes the slope vary from $20$--$60^\circ$. The slope values in the obstacle experiments represent the approximate slope created by the obstacles and not the slop of the terrain itself. In all the experiments, the rover is controlled by human operator using a game pad. The \texttt{Velocity} column in the table represents the maximum velocity limit with which the rover is set to move in the experiment. The rover can move slower than the set velocity limit but cannot exceed the limit. The \texttt{Trajectory} column in the table represents the type of trajectory followed by the rover in the experiment. The \texttt{Runs} column in the table represents the number of repetitions of the experiment conducted.

    \section{Coyote~3 Rover Platform}
    
    The Coyote~3 \gls{rwr} platform is used to evaluate the proposed \gls{cvo} approach. This section describes the rover configuration, the sensors equipped on the rover, the control method, and the onboard computation module used during the experiments. The Coyote~3 rover is equipped with four \glspl{rw} attached to four $48 V$ \texttt{RoboDrive ILM 50x08} BLDC motors combined with \texttt{HarmonicDrive CPG 17-80} gearboxes. The front wheels are attached to the main rover body and the rear wheel axle is attached to the main body through a passive bogie joint. All wheel joints, along with the bogie joint, are equipped with a \texttt{iC-Haus MU} absolute encoders. Two RGB-D cameras are mounted to both the front and rear of the rover to get the depth images and the Velodyne LiDAR, mounted in the front of the rover, is used to get the 3D point cloud data. The camera and LiDAR data are not used in the evaluation of the proposed approach in this thesis.
    
    \begin{figure}[ht]
        \centering
        \includegraphics[width=0.6\linewidth]{media/images/coyote3-component-labelled.jpg}
        \caption{Coyote~3 rover with components annotated.}
        \label{fig:annotated-coyote3-components}
    \end{figure}
    
    A \texttt{MTi-680(G)} \cite{DT-IMU-datasheet} series IMU is used to measure the linear acceleration and angular velocity of the robot body frame. The \gls{imu} sensor is located inside the main body of the rover. For the Vicon motion capture system to track the rover, four reflective markers are placed on the rover body. The Coyote~3 rover is equipped with an onboard computer with \texttt{Intel Core i7-3555LE @ 2.5 GHz} processor. The rover is controlled using an \texttt{Xbox} game pad and powered using 2 $\times$ 4500~mAh LiPo batteries. The rimless wheels are equipped with rubber feet for better friction with the ground and to reduce impact forces during wheel-ground contact. The coyote system does not have a suspension system so the forces during the contact phase of the wheels are directly felt by the IMU. The rubber foot smooths this effect. The components described in this section are annotated in Figure~\ref{fig:annotated-coyote3-components}.
