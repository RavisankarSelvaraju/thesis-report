    \chapter{Introduction} \label{chap:introduction}
        
    For any mobile robot, a reliable estimate of its pose relative to its environment is crucial for autonomous navigation. In general, mobile robots estimate their pose by integrating the incremental motion calculated using proprioceptive sensors such as \gls{imu} and wheel encoders~\cite{BOOK-Siegwart2011Introduction}. In this context, the term wheel encoder refers to the wheel joint encoder, which measures the rotation of the wheel. This pose estimation technique is called dead-reckoning based odometry. In robotic platforms with limited onboard computational power, this method of odometry is particularly important due to its simplicity and computational efficiency \cite{Agostinho2022}. This approach is vital in scenarios where positioning systems like \gls{gps}/\gls{gnss} and Motion capture systems are unavailable. Odometry estimate based on a single source of sensor measurement is susceptible to sensor drift over time and wheel slip related errors. While these errors are prevalent across mobile robots, their effects are particularly evident in the systems operating in slip-prone terrain.

    One such system is the Coyote~3 rover, shown in Figure~\ref{fig:coyote3_with_vicon_markers}. This rover uses a non-traditional wheel type, the \gls{rw}, which provides a hybrid form of locomotion that combines wheeled and legged locomotion. The spokes provide a foot-like terrain interaction while the overall wheel structure keeps the motion and control simple. This rover, due to its spoked wheels, can traverse diverse terrains like soft and coarse sand terrain, rocky terrain and inclined terrain. Unlike traditional wheeled rover, \gls{rwr} can overcome challenging obstacles effectively \cite{Brinkmann2018}.
    
    This hybrid locomotion capability of the \gls{rwr} system comes at a cost. The spokes of the \gls{rwr} system change the wheel-ground interaction from smooth rolling motion to discontinuous contact-based motion. The current baseline odometry in the Coyote~3 rover assumes a circular wheel model and uses wheel odometry for translational estimation and \gls{imu} data for orientation estimation. Since the translation and rotation are decoupled, this is commonly referred to as decoupled odometry. This approach provides a simple means of odometry estimation without complex state estimation techniques. Any slippage that occurs when traversing slip-prone terrain is not accounted for and the pose error from the slippage propagates to the odometry estimate. To address this problem, sensor fusion techniques are often employed.
    
    This project focuses on improving the proprioceptive odometry using sensor fusion techniques, specifically the \gls{inekf}. It also focuses on slip detection and rejection of wheel encoder measurements affected by slippage. Finally, a method that estimates the spokes in contact has also been developed. The remainder of this chapter is organized as the following subsections: the motivation, the contributions, and an outline of this project report.

    \section{Motivation}
    
    In harsh environment conditions like underground cave exploration, search and rescue missions in a collapse-prone building and space exploration missions, the \gls{rwr} is a suitable candidate. Such conditions often deny the possibility of external state estimation methods like \gls{gps} or Motion Capture Systems. In such scenarios, the importance of a reliable pose estimation based on proprioceptive sensors is paramount.
    
    The current baseline odometry in \gls{rwr} does not explicitly account for slip-related error. The wheel rotation, when the rover is slipping, is accounted as the change in pose of the rover while the rover is not actually moving. This leads to a significant error in the odometry estimate. This flaw makes this baseline odometry less reliable in slippery terrain like loose sand and inclined surfaces. Baseline odometry also ignores the hybrid nature of the \gls{rw} and assumes a circular wheel model with a constant wheel radius. This assumption fails to include the varying geometry of the \gls{rw} and the dynamics of making and breaking contacts. The use of only \gls{imu} data causes sensor drift in the orientation estimation and the use of wheel encoder data causes the slip related errors to be included in the translation estimation in the baseline odometry. Using a magnetometer sensor could help reduce the orientation drift in the \gls{imu}, but this method is not reliable because of magnetic interference from the motor currents Hence, a reliable odometry approach that is both slip-aware and accounts for the contact-point-based motion of the \gls{rw} remains a challenging problem for the \gls{rwr} in varied terrain conditions.
    
    \section{Contributions}
    
    This thesis aims to improve the pose estimation for the Coyote~3 \gls{rwr} system. An \gls{inekf} based sensor fusion framework is developed to fuse the \gls{imu} and the wheel encoder measurements to estimate the pose of the rover. This framework estimates the rover state using \gls{imu} data and corrects this estimation by using the wheel encoder measurements. When inconsistencies between these two sources of information occurs, the filter rejects the wheel encoder measurements based on the filter's innovation uncertainty, thereby rejecting the measurement update. The contact state of the wheel spokes can be probabilistically inferred from the innovation uncertainty.
